In 1985, researchers found a stable form of carbon that wasn't diamond or graphite. It was a cluster of 60 carbon atoms arranged in a perfect sphere. 

They named it Buckminsterfullerene after the architect Buckminster Fuller, who was famous for his geodesic domes. These 'buckyballs' opened up a new field of nanotechnology and carbon science, leading to the discovery of carbon nanotubes and graphene. It turns out nature is a fan of geometric architecture too. ⚽
